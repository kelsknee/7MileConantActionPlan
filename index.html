Housing Preservation & Vacant Land Opportunity

The study area is overwhelmingly single-family detached housing in Conant Gardens. Homeownership and long-term tenure are high, with over 60% of homeowners having lived in their home since 2010, and multigenerational households, including grandparents as primary caregivers, exceed citywide rates. Housing quality is generally stable but uneven. Renter-occupied units in two tracts show elevated rates of inadequate plumbing. THere is roughly 112 acres of vacant land (a third of it owned by the land bank) that interrupt otherwise stable blocks.

Goals

  • Help current homeowners maintain and repair their homes, with a goal towrad exterior and structural issues (roofs, porches, windows, facades) most linked to cost rather than neglect, through targeted repair funding and CDC advocacy for long-term homeowners.
  • Close the housing quality gap for renters, particularly around plumbing conditions in census tracts 5071 (16%) and 5073 (40%), where the burden falls almost entirely on renters (80%+ of affected units), through code enforcement or incentive-based programs.
  • Convert vacant parcels and structures into housing and community assets through providing residents a Land Bank Activation Toolkit for acquiring side lots, and assist Solomon Village CDC's proposed senior housing and townhome development as a neighborhood anchor.
  • Lower the barriers (cost, zoning, information) to small-scale infill housing that fits the neighborhood's single-family character, by identifying vacant lots suited to duplex, triplex, or quadplex infill and pairing this with a resident-facing tool kit item of the City's rezoning process.

Drafted from the Phase 2 housing report (census data, site visits, and community engagement). We still need to confirm this framing as a group before finalizing.

Commercial Corridor Revitalization

The stretch of East 7 Mile between Dequindre and Ryan Road is an existing hub of commercial activity, but like many Detroit corridors it experiences retail leakage, commercial vacancy, and a concentration of auto-centric or unvaried businesses. Vacant parcels along the corridor (85) currently outnumber occupied commercial spaces (59, not including Pershing High School). Residents have access to a pharmacy but not a full-service clinic, a single ATM but no bank branch, and one grocery store that opened in 2023 but does not meet the threshold of a full supermarket, so the area still carries a Low Income/Low Access designation. Public transportation along 7 Mile is served by DDOT but stops are sparse, and sidewalks, curb ramps, and lighting are inconsistent along the corridor. At the June 30 Community Input Meeting, residents were consistent about what they want to see more of: sit-down restaurants, coffee shops, medical services, family-friendly gathering spaces, entertainment options like a movie theater, and safer pedestrian connections to nearby schools.

Goals

  • Reduce parcel vacancy along the Dequindre-to-Ryan stretch of 7 Mile (85 vacant vs. 59 occupied), and fill the gaps in essential services residents lack, including a full-service grocery, a financial institution beyond a single ATM, and healthcare beyond a pharmacy.
  • Improve pedestrian and transit conditions along 7 Mile, including sidewalks, curb ramps, crossings, and lighting.
  • Create welcoming gathering spaces along the corridor that reflect the neighborhood's identity and support connection across generations.
  • Build organized business capacity over time, from an informal association toward a future Business Improvement Zone (BIZ), a special assessment that funds a board-directed budget for corridor security, marketing, and redevelopment once the association matures over 5–10 years.
  • Follow a phased implementation: construction survival and business community building (Years 1–2, via a "7 Mile Merchants Committee" and connections to Motor City Match/Invest Detroit), corridor branding and marketing (Years 1–3, facade grants and business recruitment), and street signage and wayfinding (Years 3–6, gateway signage and light pole banners).

Goals drafted from the 7 Mile Corridor Action Plan outline (Dequindre to Ryan Rd) and the Business Improvement Zone recommendation. We still need to confirm timelines and sequencing as a group.

Blight Reduction & Beautification

The Conant and 7 Mile area is a stable, cherished stretch of neighborhood blocks, and much of that stability comes from neighborhood groups, long-time residents, and the relative desirability of the housing stock. Conditions still range widely: severely blighted, boarded homes that have become targets for illegal dumping sit alongside well-kept homes with only minor needs. Residents raised concerns about flooding, limited street lighting, and a lack of greenspace for children, along with a desire for stronger property maintenance standards so that all property owners are held to the same upkeep expectations. This work also directly supports Solomon Village CDC's proposed senior complex and townhomes at the former Mason Elementary site: beautifying the blocks around that development will benefit the surrounding communities of Hawthorne Park, Conant Gardens, Northeast Central District, Pershing, and Krainz Woods, with pedestrian safety, mental well-being, and community pride extending well past the new complex's front door.

Goals

  • Reduce the number of severely blighted, boarded structures that attract illegal dumping, and repurpose vacant lots into community assets using tools like the Neighborhood Beautification Program ($5,000–$15,000 grants) and Land Bank-owned lots suited to pocket parks.
  • Address resident safety concerns tied to flooding and inadequate street lighting, improving walking conditions along routes to parks and transit in partnership with block clubs and the Department of Neighborhoods (DON) District 3 Manager.
  • Expand tree canopy, greenspace, and parks so children and families have places to gather outdoors, alongside road condition improvements, non-motorized connections, and public art where feasible.
  • Strengthen enforcement of property maintenance standards while making it easier for homeowners to stay in compliance: reduce "blight" ticket volume through resident education, connect residents to repair grants before they're cited, and support residents through the Department of Appeals and Hearings (DAH) when a ticket is disputed.
  • Extend beautification investment to the residential blocks surrounding the proposed Mason Elementary senior complex and townhomes, helping legacy homeowners nearby keep pace with exterior upkeep via the repair programs detailed in the Housing tab.

Goals drafted from the neighborhood stabilization and beautification plan and the parks/code enforcement outline. We still need to fill in ticket volume and demolition figures from our data before this goes public.
