Focus Area 01

Housing Preservation & Vacant Land Opportunity

The study area is overwhelmingly single-family detached housing (90%, vs. two-thirds citywide) in Conant Gardens, historically one of Detroit's most prominent Black homeownership neighborhoods. Homeownership and long-term tenure are high — over 60% of homeowners have lived in their home since 2010 — and multigenerational households, including grandparents as primary caregivers, exceed citywide rates. Housing quality is generally stable but uneven: renter-occupied units in two tracts show sharply elevated rates of inadequate plumbing, and roughly 112 acres of vacant land (a third of it land bank–owned) interrupt otherwise stable blocks, including the 7-acre former Mason Elementary School site.

Goals

  • Help legacy homeowners maintain and repair their homes, targeting the exterior and structural issues (roofs, porches, windows) most linked to cost rather than neglect.
  • Close the housing quality gap for renters, particularly the concentrated inadequate-plumbing conditions in census tracts 5071 and 5073.
  • Convert vacant parcels and structures — including the former Mason Elementary site — into housing and community assets instead of blight.
  • Lower the barriers (cost, zoning, information) to small-scale infill housing that fits the neighborhood's single-family character.

Strategies

  1. Legacy Homeowner Repair & Rehab AssistanceTarget funding and CDC advocacy toward exterior repairs — roofs, porches, windows, facades — for long-term homeowners, since deferred maintenance was observed even on occupied, cared-for homes.
  2. Renter Housing Quality InterventionPursue code enforcement or incentive-based programs addressing inadequate plumbing in tracts 5071 (16%) and 5073 (40%), where the burden falls almost entirely on renters (80%+ of affected units).
  3. Land Bank Activation Toolkit for ResidentsGive residents and block clubs a simple guide to acquiring side lots, turning them into gardens or gathering space, and reporting dumping or maintenance issues through Improve Detroit.
  4. Support the Mason Elementary RedevelopmentBack Solomon Village CDC's proposed senior housing and townhome development on the 7-acre former school site as a neighborhood anchor, leveraging the spillover investment senior housing typically generates.
  5. Missing Middle Infill on Scattered Vacant LotsIdentify vacant lots suited to small-scale duplex, triplex, or quadplex infill, and pair this with a resident-facing walkthrough of the City's rezoning process for parcels that need it.

Drafted from the Phase 2 housing report (census data, site visits, and community engagement) — confirm framing with your group before finalizing.

Focus Area 02

Commercial Corridor Revitalization

Everyday commercial amenities in the corridor are smaller in scale — a grocery store, laundromat, urgent care, car repair — alongside noticeable storefront vacancy. A business survey was administered along 7 Mile during site visits, though response was limited. At the Community Input Meeting, residents were consistent about what they want to see more of: sit-down restaurants, coffee shops, medical services, family-friendly gathering spaces, entertainment options like a movie theater, and safer pedestrian connections between the corridor and nearby schools.

Goals

  • Reduce storefront vacancy and fill gaps in everyday and destination amenities residents identified (sit-down dining, coffee shops, medical services).
  • Create welcoming gathering spaces along the corridor that reflect the neighborhood's identity and support connection across generations.
  • Improve pedestrian access between the commercial corridor and nearby schools and residential blocks.

Focus Area 03

Blight Reduction & Beautification

Conditions range widely across the study area — from severely blighted, boarded homes that have become targets for illegal dumping, to well-kept homes with only minor needs. Residents raised concerns about flooding, limited street lighting, and a lack of greenspace for children, along with a desire for stronger property maintenance standards so that all property owners are held to the same upkeep expectations. Participants also envisioned a stronger tree canopy and more inviting gardens and parks as part of daily life in the neighborhood.

Goals

  • Reduce the number of severely blighted, boarded structures that attract illegal dumping.
  • Address resident safety concerns tied to flooding and inadequate street lighting.
  • Expand tree canopy, greenspace, and parks so children and families have places to gather outdoors.
  • Strengthen enforcement of property maintenance standards across the neighborhood.

Strategies

  1. [Strategy 1 title]One-line description — e.g., partner with the Detroit Land Bank on priority demolition/side lot conversion for the most severely blighted parcels.
  2. [Strategy 2 title]One-line description — e.g., a street lighting improvement request tied to specific blocks residents flagged.
  3. [Strategy 3 title]One-line description — e.g., a tree canopy / greenspace planting initiative in partnership with a local nonprofit.
