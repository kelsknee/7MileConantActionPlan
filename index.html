Housing Improvements & Vacant Residential Land Opportunities

The study area is a majority single family housing, reflecting the neighborhoods history as a stable neighborhood. Over 60% of homeowners in the area have lived in their home since 2010, exhibiting both high rates of homeownership and long term tenure. This area is unique as there are increased levels of multigenerational housing, including grandparents who serve as primary caregivers, which exceed Detroit’s rate citywide. Housing quality remains generally stable but uneven. Renter occupied units in two census tracts show higher than average rates of inadequate plumbing. There is roughly 112 acres of vacant land, a third of it owned by the Land Bank, throughout the otherwise stable blocks.

  • Help current homeowners maintain and repair their homes. Prioritize exterior and structural issues like roofs, porches, windows, and facades, since cost drives these problems more often than neglect, through targeted repair funding and CDC advocacy for long term homeowners.
  • Close the housing quality gap for renters. Targeting the plumbing conditions in census tracts 5071 (16%) and 5073 (40%), where renters are most affected (80%+), through code enforcement or other programs.
  • Convert vacant parcels and structures into housing and community assets. Give residents a Land Bank Activation Toolkit for acquiring side lots, and support Solomon Village CDC's proposed senior housing and townhome development as a neighborhood anchor.
  • Lower the barriers of information that block small scale infill housing. Identify potential vacant lots for to duplex infill and pair this with a toolkit item that walks residents through the City's rezoning process.

Drafted from the Phase 2 housing report. We need to confirm this framing as a group before finalizing.

Commercial Corridor Revitalization

7 Mile between Dequindre and Ryan is a busy commercial strip. But like many Detroit corridors, it loses business to other areas, has too many empty storefronts, and leans heavily on car-focused businesses instead of a mix of shops. No business group exists yet, but starting one over the next 1 to 3 years would set the stage for a future Business Improvement Zone to fund corridor security, marketing, and redevelopment. Right now, 85 parcels are vacant along the corridor. Residents have a pharmacy but no full clinic, one ATM but no bank, and a grocery store that opened in 2023 but isn't big enough to count as a “real supermarket”, so the area still is identified as a food desert. There is a vacant, 22,500 square foot building at 4120 East 7 Mile, already zoned commercial and sitting across from Pershing High School, that could become a community education center. Bus stops along 7 Mile are few, and sidewalks, curb ramps, and lighting are spotty. At the June 30 Community Input Meeting, residents asked for sit-down restaurants, coffee shops, medical care, family-friendly gathering spots, entertainment like a movie theater, and safer routes to nearby schools.

  • Fill the empty parcels along 7 Mile between Dequindre and Ryan, where 85 empty lots outnumber the 59 spaces in use, and bring in the services residents need most: a larger grocery store, a bank, community center, and better healthcare.
  • Make walking and riding the bus safer and easier along 7 Mile by fixing sidewalks, adding curb ramps and crosswalks, and improving lighting.
  • Build welcoming spaces along the corridor that reflect who the neighborhood is and bring different generations together.
  • Grow a business organization. Start with an informal Business Association within 1 to 3 years, using help from groups like DEGC, TechTown Detroit, Detroit Means Business, and the District 3 Business Liaison. Over the next 5 to 10 years, work toward a Business Improvement Zone, a system where businesses pay a small yearly fee to fund security, marketing, and redevelopment along the corridor.

Neighborhood Beautification & Blight Reduction

The Conant and 7 Mile area is a stable stretch of neighborhood blocks, and that stability comes from neighborhood groups, community, and residents. Conditions of the area range widely, from blighted/boarded homes to well-kept properties. The corridor received 2,218 blight tickets between 2021 and 2025, mostly for overgrown vegetation, rental compliance, and waste management, and tree canopy coverage is below 25 percent across most of the neighborhoods. Residents raised concerns about flooding, limited street lighting, deteriorated sidewalks and roads, and a desire for stronger property maintenance standards so all property owners are held to the same standards. This work also supports Solomon Village CDC's proposed senior complex and townhomes at the former Mason Elementary site, and beautifying the surrounding blocks will benefit surrounding neighborhoods.

  • Reduce the number of severely blighted, boarded structures that attract illegal dumping, and repurpose vacant lots into community places. The study area contains 506 vacant residential parcels, and many sit in clusters large enough for pocket parks. Community should use tools like the Neighborhood Beautification Program, which offers $5,000 to $15,000 grants, along with Land Bank-owned lots to make this happen.
  • Address resident safety concerns tied to flooding and inadequate street lighting, improving walking conditions along routes to parks and transit in partnership with block clubs and the Department of Neighborhoods (DON) District 3 Manager.
  • Expand tree canopy, greenspace, and parks so children and families have places to gather outdoors, alongside road condition improvements, non-motorized connections, and public art where feasible.
  • Strengthen enforcement of property maintenance standards while making it easier for homeowners to stay in compliance. The corridor received 2,218 blight tickets between 2021 and 2025, mostly for weeds and vegetation, rental compliance, and waste management. Reduce ticket volume through resident education, connect residents to repair grants before they get cited, and support residents through the Department of Appeals and Hearings when they dispute a ticket.
  • Extend beautification investment to the residential blocks surrounding the proposed Mason Elementary senior complex and townhomes, helping legacy homeowners nearby keep pace with exterior upkeep via the repair programs detailed in the Housing tab.
